## Executive Decision

Keep the core book, but hedge the right risk factor.

The portfolio still has a sensible long-quality backbone: GE and JPM are cyclical-quality winners, NVDA remains the core AI long, and XLE remains a useful energy/oil hedge. The main issue is not whether to own good companies through volatility. The issue is that the current hedge does not fully match the current risk.

XLE hedges energy and oil risk. It does not properly hedge NVDA and SMH.

Portfolio action:

- Keep GE and JPM as the cleaner leadership longs.
- Keep NVDA as the preferred core AI long, but avoid duplicating too much exposure through full NVDA plus full SMH.
- Treat SMH as overlapping semi beta, not a separate independent idea.
- Keep XLE short as the energy hedge while it remains technically valid.
- Add any new hedge against the actual weak point: QQQ / semiconductor / high-beta growth exposure.
- Use weak-company shorts only as satellite hedges, not as the primary hedge.

## Hedge Framework

The right hedge hierarchy is:

1. **Primary hedge: QQQ**

QQQ is the broadest hedge against mega-cap tech, AI enthusiasm, long-duration growth, and Nasdaq crowding. It is the cleanest match if the concern is that NVDA and SMH fall with the broader technology complex.

2. **More targeted hedge: SOXX / SMH**

A semiconductor ETF hedge is cleaner if the concern is specifically semi crowding rather than broad tech. It directly offsets SMH and part of NVDA exposure, but it is narrower and can move sharply.

3. **Current hedge: XLE**

XLE is still useful, but it is a different hedge. It protects against oil / energy weakness and inflation-linked rotation. It does not solve the AI / semi beta problem.

4. **Satellite hedge: weak-company shorts**

Weak-company shorts can fall harder during volatility, but single-name short risk is real. They should be smaller than the ETF hedge and should not replace it.
